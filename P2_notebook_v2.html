0. Introduction and Tentative Findings¶

For this analysis, we were supplied a set of data from the Titanic disaster. The dataset contains demographics and passenger information from 891 of the 2224 passengers and crew on board the Titanic. For this analysis, we will assume this data is a representative sample of the population of the Titanic passengers.

In short, this analysis will show the following factors may have increased the odds of survival.

  • Female
  • First class passengers
  • Traveling with 1-3 members of the same family

1. How did gender affect survival rates on the Titanic?¶

Surviors were more than twice as likely to be female. Female survivors (233 or 68%) far outnumbered male suvivors (109 or 32%). This is represented in the pie chart below.

However, if there were more female passengers than male, this could cloud the findings. Additional analysis is needed. We should also look at survival rates for male and female passengers. This is explored in question 2.

2. How did gender affect survival rates on the Titanic? (v2)¶

In this summarization, we see that females were about 3 times more likely to survive than not. 74% (233) of all females survived the disaster, but only 26% (81) of females perished. In contrast, males were 4 times more likely to perish. 81% (468) of the males perished and only 19% (109) survived.

3. How did passenger class affect survival rates on the Titanic?¶

The following bar graphs indicate that being a third class passenger, and specifically a male third class passenger, seem to have had a negative impact on survival rates. 76% (372) of third class passengers perished. 300 of the third class passengers were male. This is in stark contrast to first class survival rates where 63% (136) of passengers survived. 97% (91) of female first class passengers survived.

4. How did family size affect survival rates on the Titanic?¶

Lastly, it appears that family size may have impacted survival rates. Passengers traveling alone or traveling with a family size of 4 or larger seems to have had a negative impact on survival rates. The survival rates exceeded the death rates only in cases where the passengers were traveling with 1, 2, or 3 family members.

5. Limitations of the Analysis¶

This analysis is limited by several factors, including the following:

Missing values: Age and Cabin contained missing values and are not included in this analysis. In particular, Cabin would have been an interesting analysis to see the relation to survival rates. It is very possible the survival rates were higher on the upper decks. However, only 204 of the 891 records contained the passenger cabin. Age and survival rate would have been another interesting analysis. But a decision would need to be made on how to handle the missing values. For age, I would simply drop the rows with missing values since a good amount of data still exists. This seems to have the least impact on mean, etc. for Age. However, this could be skewing the data in other columns. Trade-offs exist in any method chosen to handle missing data, and these should be considered carefully.

Calculations vs Statistical Tests: Interesting summary statistics should not be interpreted as "statistically significant". We can reasonably assume some of the largest differences in survival rates are also statistically significant. However, a more robust analysis would utitlize appropriate statistical tests to validate these findings and uncover insights for smaller differences in survival rates.

Representative sample: For this analysis, we will assume this dataset is a representative sample of the population of the Titanic passengers and crew. This may not be the case. There is also the possibility of outside factors that may have influenced survival rates, such as the amount time spent in the water.
